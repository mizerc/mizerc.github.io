North Cascades National Park

The North Cascades are a rugged, mountainous region in northern Washington State, forming the northernmost section of the Cascade Range. It features sharp granite spires, glaciers, deep valleys, and dense conifer forests.

In geology, spires are tall, narrow, pointed rock formations usually the result of erosion and glacial carving acting on hard bedrock, like granite.

Location#

North Cascades National Park is located in northern Washington state, in the United States. It spans Whatcom, Skagit, and Chelan counties and lies about 100 miles northeast of Seattle, near the Canadian border.

Crossing the Park thry Highway 20#

Assuming you are coming from Interstate 5 (I-5), a good road trip to see most of the park would start in Burlington and continue to Twisp via Highway 20. This route is much longer than necessary just to see the park, as the west entrance is near the town of Marblemount, reached only after many miles of driving. However, you’ll be able to see historical landmarks along the way that aren’t technically part of the park.

North Cascades National Park West Entrance#

Gorge Powerhouse#

The Gorge Powerhouse is a historical hydroelectric facility over 100 years old.

Diablo Lake#

Diablo Lake is a reservoir in the North Cascades of Washington State, known for its turquoise color caused by glacial flour suspended in the water. It’s located within North Cascades National Park and is part of the Skagit River Hydroelectric Project.

The Diablo Lake Overlook is accessible via Highway 20. From the view point you can the lake and a few mountains like the Colonial Peak.

Ross Lake#

The Ross Lake is a 23‑mile-long hydroelectric reservoir. The Ross Lake Overlook is located at 48.728275, -121.041469.

Washington Pass Vista Point#

Washington Pass Vista Point, also known as Washington Pass Overlook, sits at an elevation of about 5,477 ft (1,669 m) along State Route 20 in the North Cascades mountains.

It provides panoramic views of legendary peaks like Liberty Bell Mountain, the Early Winters Spires, the Vasiliki Ridge, as well as the winding of the Highway 20 which crosses the National Park.

There is a parking spot and you just need to walk less than a mile to access the “balcony” to be able to see the panoramic view.

Vasiliki Ridge#

A 3 mile long ridge, part of the Methow Mountains which are a sub-range of the North Cascades.

Mt Baker#

From my understanding, Mt. Baker is not officially part of the National Park, but it is on the same region. Looks like Congress excluded the mountain when they created North Cascades National Park in 1968. The second-most thermally active crater in the Cascade Range after Mount St. Helens.

Lake Shannon#

Lake Shannon is a long, narrow reservoir on the Baker River.

Artist Point#

The Artist Point is located at the very end of Mount Baker Highway. This trailhead offers seasonal hiking with views of Mount Shuksan & Baker. Artist Point typically opens in late June or early July and remains open until the first substantial snowfall of the year in early October.
